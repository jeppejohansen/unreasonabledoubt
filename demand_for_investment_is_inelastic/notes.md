# Reading List

Papers relevant to the "Demand for Investment Is Inelastic" blog post, organized by theme.

## Savings Elasticity (the core micro-foundation)

- **Cagetti (2003)** — "Interest Elasticity in a Life-Cycle Model with Precautionary Savings." *AER P&P.* Directly estimates the interest elasticity of saving in a lifecycle model. Finds it is approximately zero. The single most relevant paper for our claim.

- **Elmendorf (1996)** — "The Effect of Interest-Rate Changes on Household Saving and Consumption: A Survey." *Fed FEDS Paper.* Comprehensive survey finding little consensus that higher returns increase saving. Income and substitution effects roughly offset.

- **Cocco, Gomes & Maenhout (2005)** — "Consumption and Portfolio Choice over the Life Cycle." *Review of Financial Studies.* The canonical lifecycle portfolio choice model. Labor income acts as a bond, so portfolio allocation (not total savings) is the elastic margin.

- **Horneff, Maurer & Mitchell (2018)** — "How Persistent Low Expected Returns Alter Optimal Life Cycle Saving, Investment, and Retirement Behavior." *NBER WP 24311.* When returns fall, middle-aged workers accumulate only ~1/3 less in 401(k). Primary response is working longer and adjusting portfolio, not changing savings rate.

- **Campbell & Viceira (2002)** — *Strategic Asset Allocation: Portfolio Choice for Long-Term Investors.* Oxford UP. Framework for long-horizon portfolio choice. Total wealth accumulation is driven by savings targets; portfolio allocation is the decision variable.

## Inelastic Asset Demand and Price Impact

- **Gabaix & Koijen (2021)** — "In Search of the Origins of Financial Fluctuations: The Inelastic Markets Hypothesis." *NBER WP 28967.* $1 of inflows raises aggregate market cap by ~$5. Retirement savings flows have outsized price effects because aggregate demand for stocks is highly inelastic. The most directly relevant paper for the asset-pricing mechanism.

- **Koijen & Yogo (2019)** — "A Demand System Approach to Asset Pricing." *JPE.* Estimates asset demand elasticities from institutional holdings. Finds surprisingly inelastic demand, contradicting standard models. Micro-foundations for why flows drive prices.

- **Davis, Kargar & Li (2025)** — "Why Do Portfolio Choice Models Predict Inelastic Demand?" *JFE.* Resolves why empirical demand elasticities (~1) are three orders of magnitude lower than classical models predict (~1000s).

## Q-Investment Puzzle (why risky asset supply is inelastic)

- **Hayashi (1982)** — "Tobin's Marginal Q and Average Q: A Neoclassical Interpretation." *Econometrica.* Theoretical foundation of Q-theory of investment. Under perfect competition, average Q = marginal Q.

- **Gutierrez & Philippon (2017)** — "Investmentless Growth: An Empirical Investigation." *Brookings Papers.* Post-2000, investment has been weak relative to Q. Firms prefer buybacks over real investment. Rising concentration and governance changes explain the gap. Key paper for why equity supply doesn't respond to high prices.

- **Peters & Taylor (2017)** — "Intangible Capital and the Investment-q Relation." *JFE.* The Q-investment link improves when intangible capital is included. But intangible investment doesn't expand tradeable asset supply the same way.

- **Baker & Wurgler (2002)** — "Market Timing and Capital Structure." *Journal of Finance.* Firms issue equity when valuations are high, but the response is slow (years) and incomplete.

## Safe Asset Shortage (why safe asset supply is constrained)

- **Caballero, Farhi & Gourinchas (2017)** — "The Safe Assets Shortage Conundrum." *JEP.* The definitive treatment. Global demand for safe assets outstrips supply. Signature: secular decline in safe real rates. Few sovereigns can credibly issue safe debt.

- **Krishnamurthy & Vissing-Jorgensen (2012)** — "The Aggregate Demand for Treasury Debt." *JPE.* Treasuries carry a convenience yield. When supply increases, the yield narrows but doesn't disappear. Direct evidence that safe asset supply is constrained relative to demand.

- **He, Krishnamurthy & Milbradt (2016)** — "What Makes US Government Bonds Safe Assets?" *AER P&P.* Safety depends on market depth and sovereign credibility. Only a few governments can issue truly safe debt.

## Demographics, Savings Gluts, and Asset Prices

- **Kopecky & Taylor (2022)** — "The Savings Glut of the Old: Population Aging, the Risk Premium, and the Murder-Suicide of the Rentier." *NBER WP 29944.* Aging creates a savings glut. Simultaneously explains rising wealth-income ratio, falling safe rate, and elevated risk premium. Projects safe rate below zero through 2050.

- **Geanakoplos, Magill & Quinzii (2004)** — "Demography and the Long-Run Predictability of the Stock Market." *Brookings Papers.* Demographic cycles (baby boom/bust) drive P/E ratio cycles. When the large cohort is saving, P/E rises.

- **Mian, Straub & Sufi (2021)** — "The Saving Glut of the Rich." *NBER WP 26941.* Since the 1980s, saving by the top 1% has surged, creating massive demand for assets and driving up prices.

- **Bernanke (2005)** — "The Global Saving Glut and the U.S. Current Account Deficit." *Fed speech.* The original savings glut argument. Excess savings from developing countries flowed into US assets, pushing down rates and up prices.

- **Poterba (2001)** — "Demographic Structure and Asset Returns." *Review of Economics and Statistics.* Tests whether age structure predicts returns. Retirees decumulate slowly (bequest motives), so the savings glut is sticky.

## Other Relevant

- **Abel (2001)** — "The Effects of Investing Social Security Funds in the Stock Market." *AER.* Redirecting $1 per capita from bonds to stocks reduces aggregate capital stock by ~50 cents. Institutional savings flows have general equilibrium effects on prices.

- **Greenwald, Lettau & Ludvigson (2019)** — "How the Wealth Was Won." *NBER WP 19818.* Stock market fluctuations driven by factor share shocks, not productivity or discount rates. Consistent with quantity-of-savings-meeting-fixed-supply mattering more than return expectations.
